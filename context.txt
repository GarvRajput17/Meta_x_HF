There's a much richer landscape of techniques beyond GRPO and RLVR, extending from established production methods right through to the edges of current research. The algorithms we discussed earlier – PPO, GRPO, and DPO – are really just the entry points to a vast and rapidly evolving ecosystem.

Here’s a structured overview of the main paradigms you'll encounter, ordered by their proximity to classical RL theory:

⚙️ Reward-Based RL (Online Algorithms)
These classic RL algorithms interact with an environment (or a reward model) to collect data and actively update the policy.

PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization): The industry-standard workhorse for RLHF. A more stable policy gradient method that constrains large, potentially destructive policy updates using clipping, and typically requires a separate Critic (Value) network.

GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization): A more computationally efficient PPO variant that refuses to use a Critic network. It generates a group of outputs for each prompt, scores them, and uses the group’s average as a baseline (advantage) to determine good vs. bad responses.

DrGRPO / GSPO: Refinements addressing biases in GRPO. DrGRPO ("GRPO Done Right") removes a source of bias in the original loss function, while GSPO ("Group Sequence Policy Optimization") applies the advantage at the entire sequence level to reduce per-token optimization noise.

REINFORCE Variants: New algorithms like TOPR (Tapered Off-Policy REINFORCE) use techniques like asymmetric importance sampling for stable and efficient off-policy learning, pushing the boundaries of the classic REINFORCE baseline.

🏆 Preference Optimization (Direct Alignment)
These methods bypass the complexities of a separate reward model by directly fine-tuning the policy on human preference data.

DPO (Direct Preference Optimization): The key offline breakthrough. Rephrases RLHF as a simple classification problem on a static dataset, training directly on human preference pairs ('chosen' vs. 'rejected') for greater stability and speed.

SDPO / IRPO: Extensions of DPO providing more fine-grained control. SDPO (Selective DPO) masks unimportant tokens, while IRPO (In-context Ranking) constructs ranking lists during inference for a stronger learning signal.

🎯 Outcome-Based RL (RLVR)
The paradigm your hackathon is built on, centering on the use of deterministic, verifiable reward signals (e.g., a test harness, theorem prover) instead of human feedback or a neural reward model.

RLVR Family: Broadly includes GRPO, DrGRPO, and GSPO when used without a separate reward model.

Extension – LongRLVR: Addresses reward sparsity in long-context tasks by providing dense, verifiable rewards for intermediate steps like grounding, proving the outcome-only reward has vanishing gradients.

📦 Offline & Data-Driven RL
These techniques enable training on pre-collected static datasets of successful trajectories, eliminating the bottleneck of online data generation.

Offline GRPO: An offline version of GRPO that trains on pre-generated high-quality reasoning traces from powerful teacher models for faster, more efficient learning.

RoiRL (Reasoning with Offline Iterative RL): A lightweight family of offline methods that reformulates the problem as a weighted log-likelihood objective, avoiding the need for a reference or critic model and accelerating training.

🤝 Multi-Agent & Self-Play
Drawing directly from classical game theory, these methods train multiple LLMs (or multiple instances of one model) to compete or cooperate for emergent intelligence.

Self-Play Frameworks: Methods like SPIN (Self-Play fIne-tuNing) pit a model against previous versions of itself to generate iterative training data.

Multi-Agent RL (MARL): Used to develop meta-cognition and self-reflection by having specialized agents interact in simulation.

🛡️ Hybrid & Frontier Techniques
This category tackles the most complex challenges, especially robust reward design and other open problems.

Verifier-Guided / Composite Rewards: Combining multiple reward signals is a key frontier. Composite rewards help mitigate severe reward hacking (where an LLM garners high rewards without actually solving the task) by penalizing undesirable behaviors, ensuring that "winning" also means solving the task correctly.

Caution/Pessimism: Novel methods that penalize the model when its outputs land in "out-of-distribution" territory (i.e., produce atypical responses), actively preventing exploitation of the reward model's blind spots.

For Theme #2 and Theme #4, the challenges are very different, so the most useful RL techniques shift quite a bit. Below I map the most practical approaches from the LLM RL landscape you just saw, directly to each theme.

Theme #2: (Super) Long-Horizon Planning & Instruction Following
Core problem: Sparse rewards, deep reasoning, state tracking over thousands of tokens. The model must recover from early mistakes and plan coherently far beyond typical context windows.

Most Useful LLM RL Techniques
RLVR with Dense Process Rewards (LongRLVR‑style)

What: Instead of waiting for a single final reward, you add intermediate verifiers that score each sub‑step (e.g., “did the agent look at the right document?” or “did the plan cover all constraints?”).

Why: GRPO (or DrGRPO/GSPO) works beautifully with these per‑step rewards, because the advantage signal becomes much denser. You avoid the “zero reward always” death spiral.

Implementation: Your OpenEnv environment’s step() returns a small reward for each successful sub‑goal, not just at the end.

Offline RL with Expert Trajectories (Offline GRPO / RoiRL)

What: Collect a dataset of successful long‑horizon completions (e.g., from a powerful teacher model or human demonstrations) and use offline RL to bootstrap the agent’s policy without risky early exploration.

Why: In a 300‑instruction task, pure online RL may never see a full success. Offline pre‑training ensures the agent sees positive trajectories, from which it can then be fine‑tuned with online GRPO.

Fit: RoiRL (offline iterative RL) is particularly light‑weight and might be easy to integrate with TRL after initial SFT.

Curriculum‑Guided GRPO

What: Start with short‑horizon, heavily hinted tasks, then gradually increase the number of instructions, reduce the hints, and lengthen the episodes.

Why: Curricula guarantee that early training has a high reward probability, which is critical for GRPO to compute meaningful advantages.

Mechanism: This is an environment design pattern, not an algorithm change. You can parameterise your environment with a “difficulty level” and increment it as the reward plateaus.

Shaped Reward Functions (Multiple Rubrics)

What: Use several independent reward components that measure progress even when the final goal isn’t reached: “partial plan completeness,” “constraint violations,” “recovery attempts,” “efficiency.”

Why: Multiple signals make it harder for the model to hack a single metric, and they inject gradient information at nearly every step.

→ Best hackathon stack for Theme 2:
Start with SFT on a few expert long‑horizon demos, then apply GRPO (via TRL) with a dense process‑reward environment and a built‑in curriculum.

Theme #4: Self‑Improvement
Core problem: The agent must learn to generate its own more difficult challenges (e.g., harder math problems, stronger opponents) and improve recursively through self‑play. The goal is autonomous capability amplification.

Most Useful LLM RL Techniques
Self‑Play Fine‑Tuning (SPIN / Iterative Self‑Play)

What: The current model plays against a past version of itself (or an equally‑matched opponent). Wins and losses become the training signal. After each round, the model is updated and becomes the new opponent for the next round.

Why: This directly matches the “self‑improvement through self‑play” objective. No human labels are needed once the initial environment (e.g., a coding competition arena or negotiation game) is set.

Implementation: You can wrap SPIN‑style logic inside the training loop: the environment’s “opponent” is a frozen checkpoint of the model, and the reward is based on the outcome (win/loss/tie). GRPO can be used for the policy update in each round.

GRPO with Auto‑Generated Task Environments

What: The environment itself is dynamic: it uses an LLM (could be the same model or a separate generator) to create new, varied problems at an appropriate difficulty level. The agent then solves them, and its success/failure is used to update the policy.

Why: GRPO’s group‑relative advantage works well here, because the difficulty of auto‑generated tasks naturally varies. A completion that solves a hard auto‑generated problem will stand out against easier ones, providing a rich learning signal.

Note: The environment must be carefully sandboxed so that the task generator does not accidentally “leak” trivial tasks or reward shortcuts.

Automated Curriculum / Adaptive Difficulty RL

What: The environment monitors the agent’s success rate and adjusts the difficulty of the generated challenges (or the strength of the opponent) to keep the agent in the “zone of proximal development” — about 50% success rate.

Why: This prevents complacency (too easy) and desolation (too hard), maximizing the rate of improvement over many self‑play cycles.

Multi‑Agent RL (MARL) with Shared Policy

What: Train multiple instances of the same model to cooperate or compete in a shared environment (e.g., negotiation, resource allocation). Each agent receives rewards based on its own actions, and the experience of all agents is used to update the shared policy.

Why: Generates diverse rollouts and forces the model to learn general strategies that work against unpredictable opponents, directly fueling self‑improvement.

→ Best hackathon stack for Theme 4:
Set up a self‑play environment (e.g., a two‑player strategy game or problem‑creation arena) where the opponent is a frozen earlier checkpoint. Use GRPO to train the active agent, log the win‑rate as the reward, and automatically increase opponent strength (or problem difficulty) as the agent improves.
